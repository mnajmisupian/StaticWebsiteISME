Usability Consideration

The purpose is to raise implementers’ awareness of the usability considerations associated with enrollment and identity proofing. The goal is to promote a smooth, positive enrollment process for users by minimising user burden and enrollment friction.

General User Experience Considerations During Enrollment and Identity Proofing

  • Make each step as clear and easy as possible.
  • Clearly communicate how and where to acquire technical assistance for the users to complete their own enrollment preparation questions without outside intervention.
  • Clearly explain who is collecting their data and why.
  • Make sure all information presented is usable.
  • Conduct usability tests with representative users for each step.

1 Pre-Enrollment Preparation

An effective approach must be described to facilitate sufficient pre-enrollment preparation so users can avoid challenging, frustrating enrollment sessions.

The users will need to be provided with:

  • Information about the entire process and clear explanations of the expected timeframes.
  • Explanation of the benefits of identity proofing to allow users to understand the value proposition.
  • Information on the monetary amount and variety of acceptable forms of payment.
  • Information relevant to the sessions whether the user’s enrollment session will be in-person or in-person over remote channels, and whether a user can choose.
  • Information on the allowed and required identity evidence and the consequences for not providing the complete set of identity evidence.

3 Post-Enrollment

Actions taken immediately after enrollment before regular authenticator usage. Users were already made aware of the anticipated delivery (or pick-up) method for their authenticator at the conclusion of their enrollment session.
Usability considerations for post-enrollment include:

  • Reduce the waiting time for the arrival of users’ authenticators to allow user access to information systems and services more quickly.
  • Explain to users whether they need to pick up their authenticators in person.
  • Give users information relevant to the use and maintenance of the authenticator; (instructions for first-time use or initialization, expiration, and actions if lost or stolen).

2 Enrollment Proofing Session

Beginning of the enrollment session, remind consumers of the complete procedure. It is crucial to explicitly remind them of the usual timeframe to complete the proofing and enrollment phase. Usability considerations include

  • Offer options for rescheduling.
  • Give users a checklist to continue with the enrollment procedure. Alerted them to complete a partial identity proofing session if the information is incomplete.
  • Provide users with information on what data will be deleted, what will be kept for follow-up sessions in the future and what form of identification they need to present to complete a session in the future. Users should be given the option to opt in or out of a partial identity proofing session.
  • Set user expectations for how the enrollment session will go because prior identity verification experiences may have an impact on them.
  • Clearly state whether consumers can choose to acquire an authenticator through other means or receive one immediately after a successful enrollment process.

There are numerous needs to give users express warning at the moment of identity verification during the enrollment process.

  • Inform CSPs (Credential Service Providers) that requesting extra attributes or usage of the user's attributes may be unexpected or cause users discomfort if they are requested for any reason other than the required information.
  • Users may be reluctant or unwilling to give consent or continue the process if they do not see the benefits of the additional collection or usage.

KBV (Knowledge based verification) should not be utilised because it has severe usability issues. Given the limitations of human memory, KBV is frequently prone to errors. If utilised, take into account the following usability factors.

  • Users need context and questions that are relevant to them in order to be able to respond to KBV inquiries correctly.
  • Clarity is key when phrasing KBV inquiries, as ambiguity might result in user errors.
  • Users must be advised of the number of permitted attempts, the number of remaining attempts, adjustments based on subsequent efforts, and timeout inactivity warnings prior to timeout before they are asked KBV questions.

If an enrollment code is issued:

  • Provide users with advance notice that they will receive an enrollment code and other pertinent details.
  • Include usage instructions and the code's expiration date when delivering an enrollment code to a user.
  • Provide users with instructions on how to scan a machine-readable optical label (QR Code) if one is being distributed.
  • Remind users that if their enrollment codes expire or are misplaced before usage, they must go through the enrollment process again.
  • Give users other options besides QR codes.

At the end of the enrollment session,

  • Send users a confirmation message if their enrollment was successful and instructions on what to do next.
  • If only partially completed, advise users of the following: what information will be deleted; what information, if any, will be kept for follow-up sessions; how long the information will be kept; and what form of identification they will need to bring to a subsequent session.
  • In the event that this is unsuccessful, give users detailed information for different enrolling session types.

For both in-person and remote enrollment sessions, additional usability considerations apply:

  • Operators or attendants must describe their responsibilities to users at the beginning of the enrolling session.
  • Users should be made aware at the outset of the enrollment session that they cannot leave and that their activities must remain visible at all times.
  • Give users explicit guidance on how to complete the collection process when biometrics are gathered during the enrolling session.

Since remote identity proofing is conducted online, follow general web usability principles. For example:

  • Create a user interface that guides people through the application process.
  • Lessen the memory load on users.
  • Create a unified interface.
  • Label each stage in a sequence clearly.
  • Make it apparent where to begin.
  • Multiple platforms and device sizes are supported in the design.
  • Make the navigation consistent, straightforward to use, and easy to understand.
